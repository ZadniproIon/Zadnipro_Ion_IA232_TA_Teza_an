Observational Implications of a Plerionic Environment for Gamma-Ray Bursts

By Dafne Guetta and Jonathan Granot

Abstract

The radiation from Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) and their subsequent afterglows is predominantly explained by synchrotron and inverse Compton mechanisms. These phenomena are most comprehensively analyzed during the afterglow phase, where multi-wavelength observations provide significant insights. The theoretical framework aligns well with the observed data, supporting the fireball model. In this model, both the GRB and its afterglow result from the deceleration of a highly relativistic outflow. The emitted radiation originates from relativistic electrons within the shock-heated regions of the outflow. As per the internal-external shock paradigm, the GRB itself is attributed to internal shocks (ISs) occurring within the outflow, as demonstrated by Fenimore et al. (1996), while the afterglow arises from the external shock (ES) generated when the outflow interacts with the surrounding medium. The simplest fireball model involves a spherical shockwave expanding into a cold, uniform medium, as described by Waxman (1997), Mészáros & Rees (1997), Katz & Piran (1997), and Sari, Piran, & Narayan (1998). A notable variation considers a density profile $\rho(r) \propto r^{-2}$, which is appropriate for progenitors like massive stars surrounded by pre-supernova winds.

1 Introduction

The spectrum generated by synchrotron emission from fast cooling (FC) electrons is examined, where a continuous supply of fresh electrons is accelerated into a power-law distribution by a strong blast wave. The FC spectrum has traditionally been characterized by four power-law segments separated by three break frequencies $\nu_{sa}<\nu_{c}<\nu_{m}$, assuming a homogeneous electron distribution. However, the spatial distribution of hot electrons near the shock front and cooler electrons further downstream modifies the optically thick portion of the spectrum, introducing a new break frequency $\nu_{ac}<\nu_{sa}$ and a new spectral slope of $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{11/8}$ for $\nu_{ac}<\nu<\nu_{sa}$. The previously known slope of $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{2}$ applies only for $\nu<\nu_{ac}$. This revised ordering of break frequencies is significant for typical gamma-ray burst (GRB) afterglows in an interstellar medium (ISM). Other configurations may apply to internal shocks or afterglows in dense circumstellar winds. The implications of this spectrum for GRBs and their afterglows are discussed within the framework of the internal-external shock model. Observational evidence of $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{11/8}$ would provide insights into small-scale structures and constrain turbulent mixing downstream of the shock.

Progenitor models of gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) are categorized into two main types. The first involves the merger of compact objects in a binary system, such as neutron star–neutron star (NS-NS, Eichler et al. 1989), neutron star–black hole (NS-BH, Narayan, Pacyński & Piran 1992), or black hole–helium star/black hole–white dwarf (BH-He, BH-WD, Fryer & Woosley 1998; Fryer, Woosley & Hartmann 1999). The second category centers on the death of massive stars, including failed supernovae (Woosley 1993) and hypernovae (Pacyński 1998). These models involve the rapid formation of a black hole, with a surrounding accretion disk or torus providing a high accretion rate, driving a strong relativistic jet in the polar regions. This is referred to as the collapsar model. Another model within this category is the supranova model (Vietri & Stella 1998), where a massive star undergoes supernova explosion, leaving behind a supra-massive neutron star (SMNS) that collapses into a black hole after a few years, triggering the GRB. Long GRBs (duration $\gtrsim 2\;{\rm s}$) are typically linked to the second category, while short GRBs are associated with the first. In all these scenarios, the process concludes with a newly formed black hole accreting material from a surrounding torus, with an energy budget of $\lesssim 10^{54}\;{\rm ergs}$.

In §2, we derive the frequency distribution for an inhomogeneous electron temperature configuration. A novel self-absorption regime is identified, where the flux density scales as \(F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}\). In §3, we compute the critical frequencies and flux densities for afterglows (ESs) undergoing spherical adiabatic expansion, considering both a homogeneous external medium and a stellar wind environment. The analysis of ISs is presented in §4. Section §5 demonstrates that the early radio afterglow observations may be influenced by the newly derived spectra. We find that synchrotron self-absorption is unlikely to account for the steep spectral slopes observed in certain bursts within the \(1-10\) keV range. Additionally, we explore the potential of using these new spectra to investigate small-scale structures behind the shock front.

In this communication, we examine rapid cooling (RC), a process wherein electron populations lose energy through radiative mechanisms on timescales significantly shorter than the system's dynamical timescale, $t_{dyn}$, which marks the duration required for substantial expansion. As a result, adiabatic cooling is comparatively insignificant. The transient nature of most gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and the necessity for high radiative efficiency strongly indicate the occurrence of rapid cooling during the burst itself, as established by Sari, Narayan, and Piran (1996). Post-burst, the duration of RC extends to approximately one hour in an interstellar medium (ISM) environment, as demonstrated by studies such as SPN (Granot, Piran, & Sari 1999a), and up to one day within a dense circumstellar wind, as shown by Chevalier and Li (1999).

We posit that electrons initially possess a fraction $\epsilon_{e}$ of the system's internal energy, while the magnetic field consistently holds a fraction $\epsilon_{B}$. The analysis involves synchrotron radiation emitted by relativistic electrons accelerated by a powerful blast wave, resulting in a power-law energy distribution characterized by $N(\gamma) \propto \gamma^{-p}$ for $\gamma \geq \gamma_{m} = (p-2)\epsilon_{e}e^{\prime}/(p-1)n^{\prime}m_{e}c^{2} \cong \epsilon_{e}e^{\prime}/(3n^{\prime}m_{e}c^{2})$. Here, $n^{\prime}$ and $e^{\prime}$ denote the number density and internal energy density in the local frame, with $p=2.5$ representing the standard spectral index.

During the early rapid cooling phase of the afterglow, the system remains optically thick in the radio spectrum while being optically thin in the optical and X-ray ranges, regardless of whether the environment is an interstellar medium (ISM) or stellar wind. Consequently, the novel feature, characterized by $F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}$, should only be detectable in the radio band during the afterglow phase. In both environments, the frequencies $\nu_{ac}$ and $\nu_{sa}$ approach each other (as shown in Fig. 1) until they converge at the onset of the slow cooling (SC) phase. Subsequently, a single self-absorption break occurs at $\nu_{sa}^{(SC)}$.

A significant number of bursts exhibit a spectral slope exceeding 1/3 (or a photon number slope below -2/3) within the 1-10 keV range, as identified by Preece et al. (1998). Such steep slopes cannot be explained by optically thin synchrotron emission, as noted by Katz (1994). However, self-absorbed synchrotron emission could account for these slopes if the self-absorption frequency, ν_sa, extends into the X-ray regime. The most promising scenario for this occurs with the second type of spectral profile. Nonetheless, it is crucial to verify whether the physical conditions required for ν_sa to reach such high frequencies are plausible. This requires satisfying several constraints: (i) inverse Compton (IC) scattering must occur at smaller radii than synchrotron emission, (ii) efficient emission necessitates fast cooling (FC), and (iii) the system must remain optically thin to both Thomson scattering and pair production. The most stringent constraint is imposed by condition (iii), which can only be met under extreme parameter values: a bulk Lorentz factor Γ ≈ 10⁴ and a radiative efficiency Γ_r ≈ 50. Under such conditions, the peak of the νF_ν spectrum would occur at hν_m ≈ 1-100 GeV, unless the electron energy fraction ε_e is reduced to ≤ 10⁻², leading to a very low radiative efficiency. Given these challenges, it appears improbable that self-absorbed synchrotron emission can explain the observed spectral slopes exceeding 1/3.

2 The Pulsar Wind Bubble

Consider the spectral characteristics of an electromagnetic signal (ES) generated during the interaction of a relativistic flow with its surrounding medium. This phenomenon constitutes the primary framework for interpreting gamma-ray burst (GRB) afterglows. Specifically, we analyze an adiabatic spherical outflow encountering a cold, ambient medium characterized by a density profile $\rho(r) \propto r^{-\alpha}$, where $\alpha = 0$ corresponds to a homogeneous interstellar medium (ISM) and $\alpha = 2$ represents a stellar wind environment. The fluorescent component (FC) of the spectrum persists for approximately one hour in a typical ISM and for roughly one day in a stellar wind produced by a massive star. The post-shock proper number density and internal energy density are derived from the shock jump conditions: $n^{\prime} = 4\Gamma n$ and $e^{\prime} = 4\Gamma^{2}nm_{p}c^{2}$, where $n$ is the pre-shock proper number density and $m_{p}$ is the proton mass. Additionally, the expressions $\Delta = R/12\Gamma^{2}$, $R = 4\Gamma^{2}ct$, and $t_{dyn} = t$ are utilized, with $t$ denoting the observed time.

In a circumstellar wind environment, the number density is expressed as \( n = r^{-2} \frac{A}{m_p} \) and the column density as \( N_e = \frac{4\pi AR}{m_p} \). Interruption Spectra (ISs) are considered the primary sources of Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs). The fluctuating nature of these bursts is ascribed to emissions resulting from multiple collisions between shells within the outflow. The number of observable peaks in a burst, denoted as \( N \), corresponds approximately to the number of such interacting shells. Typically, these shells collide before their initial widths, \( \Delta_{i} \), have significantly expanded. Assuming an average initial separation between shells of \( \sim\Delta_{i} \), the initial width can be expressed as \( \Delta_{i} = cT_{90}/2N \), where \( T_{90} \) represents the burst's duration. The average energy per shell, \( E_{sh} \approx E/N \), is derived from the total energy \( E \) of the relativistic flow.

Emissions in the optically thick regime originate from the shocked material of the outer and slower-moving shells. The initial Lorentz factor of these shells is denoted by \( \Gamma_{i} \), and it decreases to \( \Gamma \) after the shock passes. The average thermal Lorentz factor of protons in this region, \( \Gamma_{r} \), equals the relative bulk Lorentz factor between the shocked and unshocked parts of the outer shell, \( \Gamma_{r} = \Gamma/2\Gamma_{i} \), and is typically of order unity. Consequently, \( e^{\prime} = \Gamma_{r}n^{\prime}m_{p}c^{2} \).

The pre-shocked fluid's number density, \( n_{i}^{\prime} \), can be estimated by dividing the number of electrons in the shell, \( N_{e} = E_{sh}/\Gamma_{i}m_{p}c^{2} \), by its volume: \( n_{i}^{\prime} = N_{e}/4\pi R^{2}\Delta_{i}\Gamma_{i} \). The number density of the shocked fluid, \( n^{\prime} \), is four times that of the pre-shocked fluid, scaled by \( \Gamma_{r} \). Post-shock, the width of the outer shell in the observer frame diminishes to \( \Delta = \Delta_{i}/8\Gamma_{r}^{2} \).

For this analysis, we adopt \( R \sim 2\Gamma_{i}^{2}\Delta_{i} \sim 4\Gamma^{2}\Delta \) as the typical collision radius between shells, and \( t_{dyn} \sim 3\Delta/c \) as the dynamical timescale. This leads to the derived relations:

In a steady shock environment, a continuous stream of newly accelerated electrons is injected immediately behind the shock front, characterized by Lorentz factors $\gamma \geq \gamma_{m}$. These electrons subsequently cool due to radiation losses. In the relativistic shock frame, the shocked fluid recedes at a velocity of $c/3$, such that $l^{\prime} = ct^{\prime}/3$, where $l^{\prime}$ represents the distance of a fluid element behind the shock, and $t^{\prime}$ is the elapsed time since the fluid passed through the shock. Just behind the shock, a thin layer exists where electrons have insufficient time to cool significantly. Beyond this layer lies a much broader region of cooled electrons. All these electrons exhibit a similar Lorentz factor, given by $\gamma(t^{\prime}) = 6\pi m_{e}c/\sigma_{T}B^{\prime}{}^{2}t^{\prime}$ or equivalently $\gamma(l^{\prime}) = 2\pi m_{e}c^{2}/\sigma_{T}B^{\prime}{}^{2}l^{\prime}$. Electrons injected earlier, which have cooled down to $\gamma_{c}$, are located at the rear of the shell, at a distance of $\Delta^{\prime} = ct_{\text{dyn}}^{\prime}/3 = c\Gamma t_{\text{dyn}}/3 = \Gamma\Delta$ behind the shock (i.e., $\gamma_{c} = \gamma(\Delta^{\prime}) = \gamma(t^{\prime}_{\text{dyn}})$). The boundary between the two layers, $l^{\prime}_{0}$, is defined as the point where an electron with an initial Lorentz factor $\gamma_{m}$ cools to $\gamma_{m}/2$:

\[
l^{\prime}_{0} = \frac{2\pi m_{e}c^{2}}{\sigma_{T}B^{\prime}{}^{2}\gamma_{m}} = \frac{3m_{e}^{2}c^{4}n^{\prime}}{4\sigma_{T}\epsilon_{e}\epsilon_{B}e^{\prime}{}^{2}}.
\]

The un-cooled layer is indeed very thin, as $l^{\prime}_{0}/\Delta^{\prime} = \gamma_{c}/\gamma_{m} \ll 1$. The position $l^{\prime}_{1}(\nu)$ is defined by $\tau_{\nu}(l^{\prime}_{1}) = 1$. The optically thin emission from $l^{\prime} < l^{\prime}_{1}$ matches the optically thick emission:

\[
n^{\prime}l^{\prime}_{1}\Gamma^{2}P_{\nu,\text{max}}\left[\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma(l^{\prime}_{1}))}\right]^{1/3} = \frac{2\nu^{2}}{c^{2}}\Gamma\gamma(l^{\prime}_{1})m_{e}c^{2},
\]

where $P_{\nu,\text{max}} \approx P_{\text{syn}}(\gamma)/\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) \propto \gamma^{0}$ and $P_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) = \Gamma^{2}\sigma_{T}c\gamma^{2}B^{\prime}{}^{2}/6\pi$ represent the peak spectral power and total synchrotron power of an electron, respectively. Since $\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) \propto \gamma^{2}$ and within the cooled layer $\gamma = \gamma(l^{\prime}) \propto 1/l^{\prime}$, equation (4) implies $\gamma_{\text{typ}} = \gamma(l^{\prime}_{1}) \propto \nu^{-5/8}$. Using equation (2), it follows that $F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}$. This new spectral regime resembles a blackbody spectrum, but with the effective temperature ($\gamma_{\text{typ}}$) varying with frequency.

The electrons, having been accelerated by the shock, experience cooling through synchrotron radiation. A critical Lorentz factor $\gamma_{c}$ is defined for an electron, which cools on the dynamical timescale $t_{dyn}$. The expression for $\gamma_{c}$ is given by:

$\gamma_{c}=6\pi m_{e}c{\big{/}}\sigma_{T}\Gamma B^{\prime 2}t_{dyn}=3m_{e}c{\big{/}}4\sigma_{T}\Gamma\epsilon_{B}e^{\prime}t_{dyn}\ ,$  (1) 

where $\Gamma$ represents the bulk Lorentz factor, $\sigma_{T}$ is the Thomson cross-section, and $B^{\prime}$ is the magnetic field strength. The Lorentz factors $\gamma_{m}$ and $\gamma_{c}$ correspond to the frequencies $\nu_{m}$ and $\nu_{c}$, respectively, using the relation $\nu_{syn}(\gamma)=3q_{e}B^{\prime}\gamma^{2}\Gamma{\big{/}}16m_{e}c$. The condition $\gamma_{c}\ll\gamma_{m}$ implies $\nu_{c}\ll\nu_{m}$, consistent with the freeze-out condition (FC).

In a uniform medium, the effective volume is expressed as $N_{e}=4\pi nR^{3}/3$. Utilizing the temporal dependence $\Gamma\propto t^{-3/8}$ (e.g., SPN), we derive the following expressions:

Our findings are predicated on the assumption of a stratified, layered structure behind the shock, consisting of a narrow, uncooled layer of width \( l^{\prime}_{0} \), succeeded by a significantly broader layer of cooled electrons with width \( \Delta^{\prime} = l^{\prime}_{0} \sqrt{\nu_{m}/\nu_{c}} \). Notably, substantial mixing would result in a homogeneous region, yielding the "homogeneous" spectrum described in SPN, and would preclude the presence of the \( \nu^{11/8} \) spectral region discussed here. A typical electron is not anticipated to traverse distances exceeding its gyration radius, \( r(\gamma) \). Calculations using the scaling relations from equations (3) for ESs and equation (4) for ISs yield \( r(\gamma_{m})/l^{\prime}_{0} = 5 \times 10^{-8}t_{2}^{-9/8}, 10^{-9}t_{hr}^{-5/4} \), and \( 5 \times 10^{-8} \), respectively. These results indicate that this mechanism cannot account for significant mixing. Another potential source of mixing is turbulence. The presence of a spectrum characterized by \( F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8} \) would signify the existence of a layered structure and constrain the effective mixing length to \( \lesssim l^{\prime}_{0} = \Delta^{\prime} \sqrt{\nu_{c}/\nu_{m}} \).

The synchrotron spectrum of a given system has been exclusively analyzed for the frequency range $\nu_{sa}<\nu_{c}<\nu_{m}$, assuming a uniform distribution of electrons (SPN, Sari & Piran 1999), where $\nu_{sa}$ denotes the self-absorption frequency. Within this framework, the spectrum exhibits four distinct power-law segments: $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{2}$, $\nu^{1/3}$, $\nu^{-1/2}$, and $\nu^{-p/2}$, progressing from lower to higher frequencies. The spectral index above $\nu_{m}$ is determined by the electron injection spectrum, where the number of electrons with Lorentz factors $\sim\gamma$ scales as $\propto\gamma^{1-p}$, and their corresponding energy scales as $\propto\gamma^{2-p}$. As these electrons cool, they predominantly deposit their energy within a frequency range $\sim\nu_{syn}(\gamma)\propto\gamma^{2}$, resulting in $F_{\nu}\propto\gamma^{-p}\propto\nu^{-p/2}$. In the intermediate regime $\nu_{c}<\nu<\nu_{m}$, all electrons contribute to the emission, as they all cool on the dynamical timescale $t_{dyn}$. Since the energy of an electron scales as $\propto\gamma$ and its characteristic frequency scales as $\propto\gamma^{2}$, the flux per unit frequency scales as $\propto\gamma^{-1}\propto\nu^{-1/2}$. The low-frequency tail of the synchrotron emission from the cooled electrons, which scales as $\propto\nu^{1/3}$, appears in the range $\nu_{sa}<\nu<\nu_{c}$. Below $\nu_{sa}$, the system becomes optically thick due to self-absorption, and the observed spectrum transitions to the Rayleigh-Jeans portion of the blackbody spectrum: 

$F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{2}\gamma_{typ}(\nu)$, 

where $\gamma_{typ}(\nu)$ represents the typical Lorentz factor of the electrons emitting at the observed frequency $\nu$. Assuming $\gamma_{typ}(\nu)=\gamma_{c}\propto\nu^{0}$, the spectrum simplifies to $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{2}$.

For an ISM environment, the synchrotron self-absorption frequency, $\nu_{sa}^{(SC)}$, remains constant over time, as it scales with $t^{0}$. Observations from current late-time radio studies indicate that $\nu_{sa}^{(SC)}$ is typically a few GHz, as reported by Tayloret al. (1998). Consequently, the entire VLA frequency range of $1.4-15$ GHz should initially fall within the regime where the flux density follows $F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}$. Early radio observations confirming this new spectral index may become feasible with upcoming HETE-era instruments.

3 X-ray Lines in the Afterglow

The shape of the FC spectrum is determined by the relative ordering of $\nu_{sa}$ with respect to $\nu_{c}<\nu_{m}$. Three distinct cases arise, with the first case, hereafter referred to as Case 1, corresponding to $\nu_{sa}<\nu_{c}<\nu_{m}$. This represents the "canonical" scenario, typically observed in afterglows within an interstellar medium (ISM) environment with reasonable parameter choices. In the optically thin regime of the spectrum ($\nu>\nu_{sa}$), the spectral characteristics of an inhomogeneous electron distribution mirror those of a homogeneous distribution. Here, all emitted photons escape the system, rendering the location of the emitting electrons inconsequential. In contrast, within the optically thick regime ($\nu<\nu_{sa}$), the majority of escaping photons originate at an optical depth $\tau_{\nu}\sim 1$, necessitating the evaluation of $\gamma_{typ}(\nu)$ at the position where $\tau_{\nu}=1$.

To this point, we have overlooked the effects of inverse Compton (IC) scattering. When $\epsilon_{e}<\epsilon_{B}$, IC scattering contributes minimally, as the power emitted via IC, $P_{IC}$, is less than that via synchrotron: $P_{IC}<P_{syn}$. However, when $\epsilon_{e}>\epsilon_{B}$, IC scattering becomes significant, with the ratio $P_{IC}/P_{syn}=\sqrt{\epsilon_{e}/\epsilon_{B}}$ (Sari, Narayan & Piran, 1996). This increased cooling results in $\gamma_{c}$ and $\nu_{c}$ decreasing by factors of $\sqrt{\epsilon_{e}/\epsilon_{B}}$ and $\epsilon_{e}/\epsilon_{B}$, respectively. Consequently, the duration of the fast cooling (FC) stage in expanding supernova remnants (ESs) is extended by factors of $\epsilon_{e}/\epsilon_{B}$ and $\sqrt{\epsilon_{e}/\epsilon_{B}}$ for interstellar medium (ISM) and stellar wind environments, respectively.

At sufficiently low frequencies, $l^{\prime}_{1}(\nu)<l^{\prime}_{0}$, which implies $\gamma_{typ}=\gamma_{m}\propto\nu^{0}$ and $F_{\nu}\propto\nu^{2}$. In a steady shock, electrons are continuously injected behind the shock with Lorentz factors $\gamma \geq \gamma_m$ and subsequently cool due to radiation losses. In the relativistic shock frame, the shocked fluid moves backward at a speed of $c/3$, so the distance of a fluid element behind the shock is $l' = ct'/3$, where $t'$ is the time elapsed since the fluid passed through the shock. Immediately behind the shock, a thin layer exists where electrons have not yet cooled significantly. Beyond this layer, a much thicker region of cooled electrons is present. All these electrons share a similar Lorentz factor, given by $\gamma(t') = 6\pi m_e c / (\sigma_T B'^2 t')$ or equivalently, $\gamma(l') = 2\pi m_e c^2 / (\sigma_T B'^2 l')$. Electrons injected earlier and cooled to $\gamma_c$ are located at the rear of the shell, at a distance $\Delta' = ct'_{\text{dyn}}/3 = c\Gamma t_{\text{dyn}}/3 = \Gamma \Delta$ behind the shock (i.e., $\gamma_c = \gamma(\Delta') = \gamma(t'_{\text{dyn}})$). The boundary between the two layers, $l'_0$, is defined as the point where an electron with an initial Lorentz factor $\gamma_m$ cools to $\gamma_m/2$:

\[
l'_0 = \frac{2\pi m_e c^2}{\sigma_T B'^2 \gamma_m} = \frac{3 m_e^2 c^4 n'}{4 \sigma_T \epsilon_e \epsilon_B e'^2}.
\]

Since $l'_0 / \Delta' = \gamma_c / \gamma_m \ll 1$, the un-cooled layer is very thin. The optically thin emission from $l' < l'_1(\nu)$, where $l'_1(\nu)$ satisfies $\tau_\nu(l'_1) = 1$, matches the optically thick emission:

\[
n' l'_1 \Gamma^2 P_{\nu,\text{max}} \left[\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma(l'_1))}\right]^{1/3} \bigg/ 4\pi = \left(\frac{2 \nu^2}{c^2}\right) \Gamma \gamma(l'_1) m_e c^2,
\]

where $P_{\nu,\text{max}} \approx P_{\text{syn}}(\gamma)/\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) \propto \gamma^0$ and $P_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) = \Gamma^2 \sigma_T c \gamma^2 B'^2 / (6\pi)$ are the peak spectral power and total synchrotron power of an electron, respectively. Since $\nu_{\text{syn}}(\gamma) \propto \gamma^2$ and $\gamma = \gamma(l') \propto 1/l'$ within the cooled layer, equation (4) implies $\gamma_{\text{typ}} = \gamma(l'_1) \propto \nu^{-5/8}$. Using equation (2), this leads to $F_\nu \propto \nu^{11/8}$. This new spectral regime resembles a blackbody spectrum but is modified because the effective temperature ($\gamma_{\text{typ}}$) varies with frequency.

The emission from the pulsar wind bubble (PWB), as calculated above, occurs at the time of the gamma-ray burst (GRB) explosion, denoted as \( t_{\rm sd} \). A critical question to address is how long the radiation from the plerion will persist following the onset of the GRB explosion. Once the supermassive neutron star (SMNS) collapses into a black hole, triggering the GRB event, the pulsar wind ceases abruptly. As a result, no new electrons accelerated at the wind termination shock are injected into the plerion after this point. The electrons within the PWB begin to radiatively cool, with adiabatic cooling becoming significant on the dynamical timescale of the plerion, \( t_{\rm sd} \) (which also serves as a good estimate of the dynamical timescale for an elongated PWB). When the last accelerated electrons cool below the Lorentz factor corresponding to a specific observed frequency \( \nu \), radiation at that frequency ceases. The cooling time is given by

\[
t_{\rm cool} = 8.9 \, (1+z)^{-1/2} \eta_{2/3}^{-3/4} \epsilon_{be,1/3}^{-1/2} \epsilon_{bB,-3}^{-1/4} \beta_{b,-1}^{9/4} E_{\rm rot,53}^{-3/4} t_{\rm sd,0}^{9/4} \nu_{9.7}^{-1/2} \; {\rm days},
\]

where the numerical coefficient applies to radio frequencies. For optical and X-ray frequencies, the coefficients are 40 minutes and \( 24 \; {\rm s} \), respectively. In our example of an elongated PWB with \( \beta_{b} \approx 1 \), the cooling time is approximately two orders of magnitude longer. Due to the strong dependence on \( t_{\rm sd} \), \( t_{\rm cool} \) can become quite large for larger values of \( t_{\rm sd} \), particularly at radio frequencies.

A more severe constraint arises from the geometrical time delay in the arrival of photons to the observer from different parts of the PWB:

$$
t_{\rm g}\sim{R_{b}\over c}=24.2\,\beta_{b,-1}t_{\rm sd,0}\;{\rm days}\ . \tag{68}
$$

This delay implies that even if the emission from the PWB were to cease immediately with the onset of the GRB, radiation from the far side of the PWB would still reach the observer for $t_{\rm g}$ after the GRB. This occurs due to the geometrical delay in photon arrival from the far side compared to the side facing the observer. For $t_{\rm sd}<t_{3}$, where $t_{3}$ is typically large [see equation (23)], the PWB is in the fast cooling regime, and most of the emission occurs near the termination shock radius, $R_{s}$. This reduces $t_{\rm g}$ by a factor of $R_{s}/R_{b}$. However, this reduction is not expected to exceed a factor of $\sim 5$ (KG). For an elongated PWB, $t_{\rm g}\sim R_{s}/c$ when $t_{\rm sd}\ll t_{\rm 3}$, while for $t_{\rm sd}>t_{\rm 3}$, $t_{\rm g}\sim R_{p}/c\approx t_{\rm sd}$. The emission from the PWB should persist for an observed time of $t_{\rm pl}\sim(1+z)\max[t_{\rm g},\min(t_{\rm sd},t_{\rm cool})]$ after the GRB. As seen from equations (67) and (68), this time is at least a few days after the GRB but can be much larger ($t_{\rm pl}\gtrsim t_{\rm g}$, see equation 68).

6.1 The Synchrotron Emission

We have derived the synchrotron spectrum for fast cooling (FC) electrons and identified three distinct spectral forms, which depend on the relative ordering of $\nu_{sa}$ with respect to $\nu_{c}<\nu_{m}$. Two of these forms exhibit a novel self-absorption regime characterized by $F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}$.

Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) and their afterglows are predominantly explained by synchrotron and inverse Compton radiation mechanisms, with the afterglow phase offering the most comprehensive observational data. The empirical evidence aligns well with theoretical models. Within the widely accepted fireball model, both the GRB emission and its subsequent afterglow are attributed to the deceleration of a highly relativistic outflow. The radiation originates from relativistic electrons accelerated in shock-heated regions. According to the internal-external shock framework, early studies (e.g., Fenimore et al., 1996) demonstrated that internal shocks within the outflow are responsible for the GRB emission, while the afterglow arises from the external shock formed as the outflow interacts with the surrounding medium. The standard fireball model involves a spherical blast wave propagating through a cold, uniform medium (Waxman 1997; Mészáros & Rees 1997; Katz & Piran 1997; Sari, Piran, & Narayan 1998). A notable modification incorporates a density profile $\rho(r) \propto r^{-2}$, which is characteristic of environments around massive stars with strong pre-explosion winds.

6.2 The SSC Emission

Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) and their afterglows are effectively modeled using synchrotron and inverse Compton emission mechanisms, with the afterglow phase providing the most comprehensive multi-wavelength observations. The observed characteristics align well with theoretical predictions. In the fireball model, both the GRB and its afterglow result from the deceleration of a relativistic outflow. Radiation originates from relativistic electrons in shocked regions. According to the internal-external shock model, Fenimore et al. (1996) demonstrated that GRBs arise from internal shocks within the flow, while afterglows stem from external shocks formed as the outflow interacts with the surrounding medium. In the simplest fireball model, a spherical blast wave expands into a cold, homogeneous ambient medium (Waxman 1997; Mészáros & Rees 1997; Katz & Piran 1997; Sari, Piran, & Narayan 1998). A notable variation involves a density profile $\rho(r) \propto r^{-2}$, typical of a massive star progenitor enveloped by pre-explosion stellar winds.

6.3 The EC Emission

In an ISM environment, the synchrotron self-absorption frequency, $\nu_{sa}^{(SC)}$, remains constant over time ($\propto t^{0}$). Observations from current radio telescopes at late times show that $\nu_{sa}^{(SC)}$ typically falls within a few GHz, as noted by Taylor et al. (1998). Consequently, the frequency range of the VLA (1.4–15 GHz) should initially lie in the region where the flux density follows the relation $F_{\nu} \propto \nu^{11/8}$. Early radio observations capable of verifying this spectral slope might become feasible in the upcoming HETE era.

For typical parameter values, only the case 1 spectrum is expected. After approximately 1 hour, slow cooling (SC) begins, and the spectrum is described by GPS.
